FFMI Calculator: How to Read Your Fat-Free Mass Index

May 13, 2026·8 min read

The Fat-Free Mass Index (FFMI) is the most honest answer to „am I muscular?". Where BMI wrongly labels muscular athletes as „overweight", FFMI only counts fat-free mass — muscle, bone, organs, water — relative to height. The result is a number that makes bodybuilders, strength athletes and recreational lifters comparable across every height class.

This guide explains the formula, the Kouri normalization, and why 25 is treated as the statistical ceiling for natural training.

The formula in three steps

  1. Fat-free mass: FFM (kg) = body weight × (1 − body fat % / 100). Example: 80 kg at 15 % body fat → 68 kg FFM.
  2. FFMI: FFMI = FFM / height in meters². At 1.80 m: 68 / 3.24 = 20.99.
  3. Normalize to 1.80 m: normFFMI = FFMI + 6.1 × (1.80 − height_in_m). Short and tall athletes now share one scale.

FFMI vs. BMI — the key difference

BMI makes no distinction between fat and muscle. A bodybuilder weighing 100 kg at 1.85 m has BMI 29.2 — flagged as „overweight" even at under 10 % body fat. FFMI solves this by removing fat mass from the equation. For a complete picture, combine body fat percentage and lean body mass with your FFMI.

How accurate must body fat measurement be?

Very accurate. A 3-percentage-point error shifts FFMI by about 0.6 — enough to move you across a category line. The methods compared:

  • DEXA scan: Gold standard, accuracy ±1–2 %. Available at sports clinics and radiology practices from around $100.
  • Hydrostatic weighing: Classic gold standard, underwater weighing, ±1–2 % — rarely available outside research labs.
  • Calipers (skinfold): 3- or 7-site method, ±3–5 %, operator-dependent.
  • Bioimpedance scales: ±3–8 %, drift with hydration and meals. Measure at the same time of day.

Using FFMI in your training plan

FFMI is a trend marker — it shows over months and years whether your training is building real muscle. Three hypertrophy levers matter most: progressive overload (more weight or reps), 1.6–2.2 g of protein per kg bodyweight, and a moderate caloric surplus. With your daily energy needs known, eating 200–500 kcal over maintenance keeps fat gain in check while muscle grows.

Realistic expectations: beginners add 1.5–2.5 FFMI points in year one. Advanced lifters add 0.3–0.8 per year. Going from FFMI 21 to 24 is at least a three-year project — and every step closer to 25 gets exponentially harder.
